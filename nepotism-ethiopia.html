01 Understanding Nepotism

What is Nepotism?

Before examining its effects in Ethiopia, it is essential to establish a clear understanding of what nepotism means and how it manifests in different contexts.

"nep·o·tism"

Nepotism is the practice of favouring relatives or close associates in professional matters — particularly in hiring, promotion, and resource allocation — based on personal relationships rather than merit, qualifications, or performance. It directly undermines fair competition and meritocracy in the workplace.

👨‍👩‍👧

Familial Nepotism

Appointing or favouring immediate family members — children, siblings, spouses — regardless of their qualifications for a role.

🤝

Political Patronage

In Ethiopia, civil service recruitment has been closely tied to ruling party membership and political allegiance, a form of organised nepotism.

🏘️

Ethnic / Clan-based

Preferential hiring based on ethnic group or clan identity — a particularly prominent form of nepotism in Ethiopia's ethnically diverse society.

💼

Cronyism

Favouring close friends or allies — sometimes called "God-fatherism" in Ethiopian public administration — over qualified outsiders.

A Federal System Vulnerable to Favouritism

Ethiopia operates a multi-layered federal civil service — federal, regional, and local government levels — each with its own hiring practices. This fragmentation creates pockets where oversight is weak and personal connections dominate recruitment decisions.

Political Party Affiliation as a Hiring Criterion

Research consistently shows that recruitment into Ethiopia's civil service has often been contingent upon affiliation with the ruling political party. This transforms what should be a merit-based system into one where loyalty outweighs competence.

Ethnic and Clan Identity in Hiring

In regions like the Somali Region, clan-based favouritism has historically defined who receives public sector positions. Reports documented cases where regional leaders appointed nine cousins as bureau heads — concentrating power within a single family.

Low Salaries and the Corruption Cycle

Low public sector wages push officials toward corrupt practices, including nepotistic hiring, as a form of compensation or security. Poverty and nepotism reinforce each other in a self-perpetuating cycle that is difficult to break without systemic reform.

03 Historical Timeline

Nepotism in Ethiopia:
A Historical Pattern

Nepotism in Ethiopian governance is not a new phenomenon. Understanding its history helps explain why it remains so deeply embedded today.

1960s

Emperor Haile Selassie Establishes Central Personnel Agency

Created to unify recruitment under merit-based principles — but in practice, imperial favour and aristocratic connections shaped who received senior appointments.

1974–1991

Derg Regime: Political Loyalty Over Competence

The military junta replaced merit with ideological alignment. Cadres loyal to the Marxist regime received preferential employment, establishing a precedent of political patronage in hiring.

1991–2018

EPRDF Era: Ethnic Federalism and Party Patronage

The ethnic federalism system, while designed to address diversity, also enabled ethnically based favouritism. Civil service positions were heavily linked to EPRDF party membership and regional ethnic identity.

2001

Anti-Corruption Agencies Established

Ethiopia created centralised anti-corruption agencies including the Federal Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (FEACC). Enforcement has remained weak, however, with nepotism persisting across public institutions.

2018–Present

Reform Era Under PM Abiy Ahmed

Reforms have improved some procurement processes and introduced greater oversight. However, research shows nepotism and political patronage continue to undermine meritocratic hiring, with over 80% of civil servants acknowledging the role of personal connections.

05 Consequences

The Ripple Effects
of Nepotism

Nepotism in employment is not a victimless practice. Its consequences reach far beyond the individual — shaping economies, institutions, and societies.

IMPACT 01

Qualified Candidates Are Excluded

When positions are allocated based on connections rather than merit, highly qualified individuals — especially young graduates — are systematically shut out of the job market. This is particularly severe in Ethiopia's public sector where over 80% of hiring is influenced by personal ties.

Individual Harm
IMPACT 02

Public Service Quality Deteriorates

When less competent individuals are hired due to connections, the quality of public services declines. In Ethiopia, this manifests in weakened healthcare, education, and administrative systems — directly affecting millions of citizens who depend on these services.

Institutional Harm
IMPACT 03

Youth Unemployment and Social Instability

With urban youth unemployment at 19.4%, nepotism contributes to hopelessness among educated youth who cannot enter the formal workforce. Research links prolonged youth unemployment to increased crime, migration, and social unrest.

Social Harm
IMPACT 04

Economic Productivity Loss

Economies grow fastest when the most capable individuals occupy the most productive roles. Nepotism misallocates human capital — placing the connected rather than the competent in key economic positions, dragging down Ethiopia's growth potential.

Economic Harm
IMPACT 05

Erosion of Public Trust

When people believe that success depends not on hard work but on who you know, trust in institutions collapses. Already, a third of Ethiopians perceive public officials as corrupt — undermining the legitimacy of the state.

Democratic Harm
IMPACT 06

Brain Drain and Emigration

Talented Ethiopians, finding advancement blocked by nepotistic barriers, increasingly seek opportunities abroad. This "brain drain" deprives Ethiopia of the human capital needed for long-term development — a self-defeating cycle driven by corruption.

National Harm
06 Case Studies

Documented Cases

Academic research and investigative reporting have identified concrete instances of nepotistic employment practices across Ethiopia's regions and sectors.

Somali Region · Regional Government

Nine Cousins as Bureau Heads

A former regional president was documented to have appointed nine of his cousins to head the region's most important government bureaus. Government money was reportedly distributed as political patronage to loyalists and corrupt officials — a stark example of clan-based nepotism at the highest levels.

Addis Ababa · Land Administration

"Nearly Impossible Without Bribery"

The Federal Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (FEACC) itself reported that obtaining a plot of land in Addis Ababa was "nearly impossible" without bribing city administration officials — revealing how nepotism and corruption interlock to exclude ordinary citizens from economic opportunities.

Federal Civil Service · Nationwide

The Shift Away From Meritocracy

A major academic study of Ethiopian public administration found that recruitment has "largely shifted from the principles of meritocracy toward factors such as nepotism, political patronage, God-fatherism, and ethnicity." Senior positions became subject to inter-party bargaining rather than merit.

Ambo Town · Local Government

Nepotism as Normalised Practice

A case study of Ambo Town found nepotism endemic in public sector hiring, with fraud, bribery, and abuse of power documented as "principal features of corruption." Low salaries were found to drive public officials toward nepotistic practices as economic coping strategies.

"

Practice demonstrates that the basis for recruitment into the Ethiopian public sector has largely shifted from meritocracy — educational qualification and experience — toward factors such as nepotism, political patronage, God-fatherism, and ethnicity.

08 Solutions & Recommendations

Paths Toward Fairness

Addressing nepotism requires systemic reforms at multiple levels. Research and global best practices point to the following evidence-based solutions.

Mandatory Competitive Examinations

Expanding the use of formal, standardised examinations for all civil service positions — not merely 20% of ministries — creates an objective, transparent hiring benchmark that reduces the influence of personal connections.

Independent Recruitment Bodies

Establishing autonomous hiring commissions, insulated from political interference and party loyalty pressures, can depoliticise recruitment and ensure positions go to the most qualified candidates.

Transparent Procurement and Job Advertising

Requiring all government positions to be publicly advertised through media, with standardised application processes, reduces the informal networks through which nepotistic hiring operates.

Strengthened Anti-Corruption Enforcement

Ethiopia's FEACC and related agencies need greater independence, resources, and legal powers to investigate and prosecute nepotistic hiring — with meaningful consequences for violators.

Living Wages for Public Officials

Addressing the low-salary root cause: when public officials are paid adequately, the economic pressure that drives nepotistic practices as a form of family security is significantly reduced.

Civil Society and Media Oversight

Empowering a free press and civil society organisations to scrutinise public sector hiring decisions creates accountability mechanisms that complement formal enforcement structures.

09 Conclusion

What the Research Tells Us

The research question — to what extent does nepotism affect employment in Ethiopia? — yields a clear and troubling answer. Nepotism affects Ethiopian employment extensively and systemically. With over 80% of civil servants acknowledging the role of personal connections, only 20% of ministries using merit-based examinations, and urban youth unemployment at 19.4%, the evidence points to a labour market where who you know routinely matters more than what you can do.

This is not merely an abstract injustice. It has real consequences: qualified young Ethiopians shut out of decent work; public services delivered by underqualified appointees; trust in institutions eroded; and economic potential squandered. Nepotism is not a marginal phenomenon — it is embedded in Ethiopia's administrative DNA, with roots stretching back through every political era.

Yet the path forward is clear. Where meritocracy has been strengthened — through transparent hiring, competitive examinations, and genuine oversight — outcomes improve. Ethiopia possesses the legal frameworks and institutional architecture for reform. What is needed is the political will to enforce them, and an informed citizenry that demands accountability.
